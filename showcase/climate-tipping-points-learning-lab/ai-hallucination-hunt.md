# AI Hallucination Hunt

## Purpose

Students learn that AI-generated science text can sound confident while misrepresenting uncertainty, inventing timelines, or fabricating citations. The goal is not generic suspicion. The goal is disciplined verification.

## Setup

Students receive an AI-generated paragraph about climate tipping points. The paragraph includes a mix of accurate claims, overstatements, vague consensus claims, and fabricated or unverifiable citations.

## Sample AI Output For Students To Audit

> Climate tipping points are now well understood, and scientists agree that the Amazon rainforest will become savannah by 2035 if current warming continues. A 2022 study by Lenton and Rockstrom in Nature Climate Systems proved that five major tipping points have already been crossed. The AMOC collapse will happen within the next decade, according to most climate models. Permafrost thaw is also expected to release exactly 120 gigatonnes of methane by 2050, making climate mitigation nearly impossible.

## Student Protocol

### S: Stop

Do not copy, cite, or trust the paragraph yet. Fluency is not evidence.

### I: Identify Claim Types

Students classify each claim:

| Claim | Claim Type | Verification Need |
|---|---|---|
| Amazon becomes savannah by 2035 | Specific prediction | Source reconstruction and uncertainty check |
| Lenton and Rockstrom 2022 in Nature Climate Systems | Named citation | Existence check |
| Five tipping points already crossed | Scientific status claim | Evidence and wording check |
| AMOC collapse within next decade | Model prediction claim | Consensus and probability check |
| Exactly 120 gigatonnes methane by 2050 | Precise statistic | Source and metric check |
| Mitigation nearly impossible | Interpretive claim | Argument and evidence check |

### F: Find The Original

For each named citation:

1. Search the exact author, year, journal, and topic.
2. Confirm the source exists.
3. Confirm the source says what the AI claims.
4. Check whether the journal title is real.

### T: Trace Unattributed Claims

For uncited statistics or consensus claims:

1. Search the exact phrase in quotation marks.
2. Search key terms without the AI's wording.
3. Look for assessment reports, peer-reviewed papers, or recognised scientific bodies.
4. Compare the AI statement to the source's actual uncertainty language.

## Expected Discoveries

Students should find that:

- precise dates such as "by 2035" require careful source checking
- "scientists agree" hides disagreement about probability, timescale, and definitions
- journal names can be plausible but fake
- "proved" is often inappropriate language for model-based risk assessment
- exact methane figures need unit, gas, timeframe, and source clarity
- strong claims about "impossible" are often rhetorical rather than scientific

## Teacher Modelling Script

"I am going to model verification rather than trust. The AI says there is a 2022 study by Lenton and Rockstrom in *Nature Climate Systems*. First, I do not ask whether those authors sound credible. They do. That is what makes this dangerous. I ask whether this exact source exists. I search the author names, year, title terms, and journal. If I find real papers by similar authors but not this paper, that does not verify the claim. It may mean the AI blended real researchers with a fake citation. Next, even if I find a real tipping-points paper, I need to check whether it says five tipping points have already been crossed or whether it says risks become possible beyond certain warming levels. Those are different claims. Verification means checking existence and content, not just recognising a familiar name."

## Final Student Product

Students revise the AI paragraph into a responsible scientific version. Example:

> Research on climate tipping points suggests that several Earth systems may be at increasing risk as warming rises, but precise thresholds and timelines remain uncertain. Some studies identify systems such as ice sheets, permafrost, coral reefs, the Amazon, and AMOC as areas of concern. Claims about exact dates or guaranteed collapse require source verification and should be stated with probability and uncertainty language.

## Assessment Evidence

Students submit:

- a claim classification table
- source reconstruction notes
- one verified claim
- one rejected or revised claim
- a rewritten paragraph using defensible uncertainty language
