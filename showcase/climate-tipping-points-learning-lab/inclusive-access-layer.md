# Inclusive Access Layer

## Design Principle

The learning lab keeps cognitive demand high while changing access routes. Students still reason about nonlinear systems, evidence, AI reliability, and bounded action. The adaptations reduce unnecessary barriers in language, representation, working memory, and participation.

## Anticipated Barriers

| Barrier | Risk | Design Response |
|---|---|---|
| Dense scientific vocabulary | Students cannot access the concepts behind unfamiliar terms | Tier vocabulary and pre-teach high-utility terms |
| Abstract systems language | Students repeat terms without causal reasoning | Use variable cards, loop arrows, and sentence frames |
| Graph interpretation load | Students miss the feedback concept because axes and data are difficult | Separate graph-reading mini-lessons from systems reasoning |
| Climate anxiety | Students shut down or move into despair | Use bounded agency and adult responsibility framing |
| AI overreliance | Students outsource thinking | Retrieve-first and unassisted checkpoints |
| English academic language demands | Multilingual learners know concepts but cannot express them fluently | Provide sentence frames without reducing reasoning |
| Participation imbalance | Confident students dominate systems mapping | Use roles: evidence checker, loop tracer, uncertainty monitor, agency mapper |

## Vocabulary Tiers

| Tier | Words | Teaching Approach |
|---|---|---|
| Everyday / bridge | change, risk, stable, sudden, connected, pressure | Use in initial discussion and analogies |
| Academic | threshold, resilience, uncertainty, variable, causal, evidence, inference | Explicitly teach and revisit |
| Technical | albedo, AMOC, permafrost, hysteresis, positive feedback, carbon sink | Teach through diagrams, examples, and retrieval |

## Sentence Frames

### Feedback Explanation

- When **[variable A]** increases/decreases, **[variable B]** changes because **[mechanism]**.
- This creates a reinforcing loop because **[later change]** increases/decreases **[starting variable]**.
- This is positive feedback in the scientific sense because **[it reinforces the direction of change]**.

### Uncertainty Language

- The evidence suggests **[claim]**, but the timing is uncertain because **[reason]**.
- A more defensible version of the AI claim is **[revision]**.
- The source supports **[specific claim]**, but not **[overclaim]**.

### Bounded Agency

- Students can directly control **[action]**.
- Students may influence **[people/system]** by **[method]**.
- Responsibility for **[larger action]** belongs primarily to **[adult/institution]**.
- A possible unintended consequence is **[risk]**, so the design should **[safeguard]**.

## Representation Options

Students may produce:

- written systems brief
- annotated diagram with oral defence
- slide deck with evidence notes
- short video explanation with source appendix
- mixed media map plus teacher interview

All options must include the same evidence requirements and oral defence.

## Emotional Safety And Agency

Climate tipping points can produce dread. The unit avoids both denial and despair by making three distinctions explicit:

1. **Understanding is not blame.**
2. **Concern is not the same as control.**
3. **Student action should be bounded, supported, and honest about power.**

## Non-Negotiables

Inclusive adaptation must not:

- remove feedback-loop reasoning
- replace evidence verification with trust
- turn climate action into individual guilt
- allow AI to do the learner's independent work
- mark language fluency as if it were conceptual understanding
