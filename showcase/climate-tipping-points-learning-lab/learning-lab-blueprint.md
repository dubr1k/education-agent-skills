# Learning Lab Blueprint

## Unit Title

**Climate Tipping Points: Systems, Evidence, AI, And Responsible Action**

## Duration

Four weeks, 12 lessons, plus one final showcase / defence session.

## Student Level

Year 12 / first-year undergraduate.

## Enduring Understandings

Students will understand that:

1. **Climate tipping points are nonlinear system changes where feedback loops can make gradual pressure produce sudden, self-reinforcing shifts.**
2. **Scientific uncertainty does not mean ignorance; it often describes probability, confidence, timescale, and model limits.**
3. **AI can accelerate scientific learning only when learners verify claims, preserve their own cognitive work, and check independent transfer.**
4. **Responsible action in complex systems requires bounded agency: knowing what students can control, influence, investigate, or escalate to adults and institutions.**

## Essential Questions

1. When does gradual change become system transformation?
2. How can we reason responsibly when evidence is strong but future thresholds remain uncertain?
3. What does AI help us see, and what can it make us falsely believe we understand?
4. What kind of action is appropriate when a system is larger than our individual control?

## Knowledge Architecture

| Knowledge Type | Students Need |
|---|---|
| Factual | Tipping point, threshold, positive feedback, negative feedback, albedo, permafrost, AMOC, Amazon dieback, ice-sheet instability |
| Conceptual | Nonlinearity, feedback amplification, resilience, hysteresis, uncertainty, model confidence |
| Procedural | Interpret a feedback-loop diagram, read a threshold graph, trace claims to evidence, construct a systems map |
| Epistemic | Distinguish evidence, model, inference, uncertainty, speculation, and AI hallucination |
| Ethical / civic | Bound agency, avoid blame, consider stakeholders, anticipate unintended consequences |

## Four-Week Sequence

| Week | Focus | Core Skills | Student Product |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | What is a tipping point? | retrieval, worked examples, systems iceberg | Personal misconception map and feedback-loop explanation |
| 2 | How do tipping systems interact? | hexagon mapping, source evaluation, AI hallucination hunt | Evidence-backed systems map |
| 3 | How should AI support learning here? | cognitive tutor, hint ladder, teach-back, transfer | Tutor-supported and unassisted evidence record |
| 4 | What action is responsible? | leverage design, agency circles, assessment defence | Local systems response proposal |

## Lesson Snapshot

### Lesson 5: Is The Amazon A Carbon Sink, A Carbon Source, Or A System Near A Threshold?

**Opening retrieve-first prompt:**  
"Without notes, explain what a climate tipping point is and give one example. Rate your confidence from 0-100."

**Phenomenon:**  
Students examine two short data excerpts: forest loss trends and drought/fire feedbacks in the Amazon.

**Core task:**  
In pairs, students build a feedback-loop explanation connecting:

- deforestation
- rainfall recycling
- drought stress
- fire risk
- carbon release
- regional climate change
- biodiversity loss

**AI tutor constraint:**  
The AI may ask questions and give progressive hints. It may not produce a completed feedback-loop explanation until the student has attempted one.

**AI-literacy move:**  
Students test one AI claim: "The Amazon will definitely become savannah by 2035." They classify the claim, trace evidence, and revise it into a defensible scientific statement.

**Exit evidence:**  
Students submit:

1. one feedback loop they can explain
2. one uncertainty they can name precisely
3. one AI-generated claim they verified or rejected
4. confidence before and after

## Final Performance Task

**Task:**  
Create and defend a **Climate Tipping Point Systems Brief** for a school, community, or local council audience.

The brief must include:

1. a clear explanation of one climate tipping system
2. a systems map showing feedback loops and leverage possibilities
3. evidence from at least three credible sources
4. an AI audit log showing how AI-generated claims were checked
5. a bounded action recommendation distinguishing control, influence, concern, and adult/institutional responsibility
6. a reflection comparing scaffolded AI performance with unassisted performance

## Teacher Role

The teacher acts as:

- curriculum guardian
- evidence-quality coach
- systems facilitator
- emotional safety monitor
- final assessor of judgement and transfer

The AI acts as:

- retrieval coach
- hint manager
- misconception detector
- verification partner
- formative evidence logger

The AI does not act as:

- final authority on science
- provider of unverified claims
- replacement for teacher judgement
- shortcut around student thinking
